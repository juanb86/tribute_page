Dr René Favaloro

He was born on July 14, 1923 in La Plata. The son of a carpenter and dressmaker, he lived a poor childhood in El Mondongo, an immigrant neighborhood. After graduating in 1949, he practiced as a rural doctor in Jacinto Aráuz, La Pampa, for twelve years. Married to María Antonia, his high school girlfriend. In January 1962, he entered the Cleveland Clinic, in the United States, where he remained until June 1971. There he developed myocardial revascularization surgery, or the bypass technique, which made his prestige transcend the limits of that country. Favaloro made 13,000 by-passes until his 69 years, when he decided to dedicate himself entirely to teaching. He returned in 1971, with the idea of ​​developing a center that combined medical care, research and education. With this objective, the Favaloro Foundation was created in 1975. In 1980, he founded the Basic Research Laboratory that depended on the Favaloro Foundation's Research and Teaching Department. Subsequently, it became the Basic Sciences Research Institute of the University Institute of Biomedical Sciences, which, in turn, led in August 1998 to the creation of the Favaloro University. In 1992, the Favaloro Foundation Institute of Cardiology and Cardiovascular Surgery was inaugurated in Buenos Aires. He was an active member of 24 societies, corresponding to 4, and honorary of 42. He received countless international distinctions among which stand out: the 1979 John Scott Prize, awarded by the city of Philadelphia, USA; the creation of the "Dr René G. Favaloro" Chair of Cardiovascular Surgery (Tel Aviv University, Israel, 1980); the distinction of the Fundación Conchita Rábago de Giménez Díaz (Madrid, Spain, 1982); the Argentine Master of Medicine award (1986); the Distinguished Alumnus Award from the Cleveland Clinic Foundation (1987); The Gairdner Foundation International Award, awarded by the Gairdner Foundation (Toronto, Canada, 1987); the René Leriche Prize 1989, awarded by the International Society of Surgery; the Gifted Teacher Award, awarded by the American College of Cardiology (1992); the Golden Plate Award from the American Academy of Achievement (1993); the Prince Mahidol Prize, awarded by His Majesty the King of Thailand (Bangkok, Thailand, 1999). He killed himself on Saturday, July 29, 2000 at his home in the city of Buenos Aires by shooting himself in the heart. The tragic decision would have been the outbreak of a serious depression caused by the economic and financial difficulties faced by the Foundation that he presided over, aggravated by the lack of sensitivity and responses on the part of various medical entities, both public and private, that maintained unpaid debts due to the sum of 18 million dollars with your institution.
